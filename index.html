Notes of Bezout's Identity

Posted on 26-07-2024 In Notes

Content

Theorem 1

Let $a,b \in \Z$, where $\gcd(a,b)=d$, then $\forall x,y \in \Z$, $ax+by$ must be a multiple of $d$. Especially, $\exist x,y \in \Z$, where $ax+by=d$.

Theorem 2

The equation $ax+by=1$ has integer solution iff $gcd(a,b)=1$.

Proof

Proof to Theorem 1

Let $d=\gcd(a,b)$

Apparently, $d \mid a$ and $d \mid b$.

$\implies d \mid ax+by$ , where $x,y \in \Z$

$\implies ax+by=dk$, where $k \in \Z^+$

Especially when $k=1$, $ax+by=d$. $\square$

Proof of Theorem 2

$ax+by=1$ has integer solution $\implies$ $gcd(a,b)=1$

It is not difficult to note that Theorem 2 is a special case of Theorem 2, where $d=1$.

Proof,

Assume $a,b$ are not co-prime, where $\gcd(a,b) \not = 1$.

$\implies d \not = 1$

According to Theorem 1 and the equation, we can deduce that $ax+by=kd = 1$, where $d=gcd(a,b)$ and $k \in \Z^+$. Since both $k$ and $d$ are integer, therefore $d=1$, which forms a contradiction with the proposition we made above. $Q.E.D.$

$gcd(a,b)=1$ $\implies$ $ax+by=1$ has integer solution

Omitted, since quite obvious proof by applying Theorem 1.
